What the grant allows

  • Case: supplier bank-change review only.
  • Agents: requester, evidence, risk, reviewer, action.
  • Safe fields: invoice summary, vendor record hash, policy excerpt, risk memo.
  • Human grants scope: today’s review, not future access.
Never released to shared room: raw account number, routing number, secret canary, or reusable credential material.

Why a room needs a key

A shared room coordinates work. It should not become a permission boundary by accident.

The moment

A supplier wants to change where money gets sent.

Finance wants help from an evidence agent, a risk agent, reviewers, and an action agent. That is good room work. It is also exactly where raw bank details should stay scoped.

Without RoomKey

The room becomes authority by accident.

An agent asks, another agent shares, and the transcript may now hold material that should have stayed scoped.

With RoomKey

The human grant becomes the key.

The room discusses safe summaries first. Sensitive context waits for scoped approval. The run ends with a receipt a judge can check.

How it feels to use

It should feel like approving a risky operation, not editing a schema.

Before approval

ScopeGate says no.

“I can help with the review, but I need approval for this case before sensitive banking context can be released.”

Human approval

The manager says yes, narrowly.

“Approve this supplier review for today. Let the evidence, risk, reviewer, and action agents use the invoice summary, vendor record hash, policy excerpt, and risk memo. Do not post raw account details.”

During review

Agents can work.

The room sees safe summaries, hashes, decisions, and gate state. Sensitive values stay out of the shared transcript.

After revocation

The key stops working.

Late replay does not recover protected context. The run is sealed for audit.
